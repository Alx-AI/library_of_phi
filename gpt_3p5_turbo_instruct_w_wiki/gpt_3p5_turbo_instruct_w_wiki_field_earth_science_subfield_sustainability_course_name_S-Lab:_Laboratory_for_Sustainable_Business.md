This means that a sustainable business not only considers its financial success, but also its impact on the environment and society. It aims to minimize its negative impact and potentially even have a positive effect on the global or local environment, community, society, or economy.



To be considered a sustainable business, a company must meet four criteria: it must participate in environmentally friendly or green activities, ensure that all processes and products address current environmental concerns, maintain profitability, and use renewable resources. This definition highlights the importance of balancing economic success with environmental and social responsibility.

Sustainable businesses aim to create value for customers, investors, and the environment, while also meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.



However, achieving sustainability in business is not an easy task. It requires a structural approach and a commitment to balancing the needs of all stakeholders. This can be challenging in a world where short-term profits often take precedence over long-term sustainability. But the consequences of inaction are too great to ignore.

These resources are often limited and can be depleted if not managed properly. Therefore, it is important for businesses to collaborate and work together to ensure the sustainability of these shared resources.



One approach to achieving this is through supply chain collaboration. This involves different forms and approaches, such as collaborative communication, collaborative execution, and open supply chain innovation. Collaborative communication refers to the contact and message transmission process among supply chain partners, which should be open, frequent, balanced, two-way, and multilevel in order to indicate close inter-firm relationships. This allows for better coordination and management of shared resources.



Collaborative execution, on the other hand, focuses on the process of executing supply chain transactions in a collaborative manner. This includes working with suppliers to ensure the right quantity of materials is delivered in a timely manner. By closely collaborating and communicating, businesses can reduce waste and inefficiencies in the supply chain, leading to a more sustainable use of shared resources.



Another important aspect of ensuring the sustainability of shared resources is through open supply chain innovation. This approach is inspired by the concept of open innovation, where supply chain networks can facilitate innovation platforms for collaboration and co-creation among n-tier supplier chain actors and partners beyond the supply chain network. By working together, businesses can develop more sustainable practices and solutions for managing shared resources.



In addition to collaboration, coordinating contracts can also play a role in ensuring the sustainability of shared resources. These contracts provide incentives for all members of the supply chain to behave in a way that is aligned with sustainability goals. By specifying contract parameters such as quantity, price, quality, and deadlines, businesses can improve supplier-buyer relationships and promote more sustainable practices.



Information sharing is another key factor in ensuring the sustainability of shared resources. By sharing relevant, accurate, complete, and confidential information with supply chain partners in a timely manner, businesses can improve coordination and decision making. This can lead to more efficient use of shared resources and a reduction in waste.



Lastly, joint decision making is crucial in managing shared resources sustainably. This refers to the process of making decisions together with supply chain partners, rather than in isolation. By involving all parties in the decision making process, businesses can ensure that the use of shared resources is sustainable and beneficial for all involved.



In conclusion, the sustainability of shared resources is a crucial aspect of achieving sustainability in the supply chain. By collaborating, communicating, and making joint decisions, businesses can ensure the responsible and sustainable use of shared resources. This not only benefits the environment, but also contributes to a better future for all.

### Section: 10.3 Action at the Macro Level: Setting a Price for Carbon:



Carbon pricing is a crucial tool in the fight against climate change. By placing a price on carbon emissions, businesses and individuals are incentivized to reduce their carbon footprint and transition to more sustainable practices. In this section, we will explore the importance of carbon pricing at the macro level and its role in creating a more sustainable future.



#### 10.3a Importance of Carbon Pricing



Carbon pricing is essential for achieving significant and rapid reductions in greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. It creates a financial incentive for businesses and individuals to reduce their carbon footprint, as they will face higher costs for emitting carbon. This, in turn, encourages the adoption of cleaner and more sustainable practices, such as investing in renewable energy sources and implementing energy-efficient technologies.



One of the most effective ways to implement carbon pricing is through a carbon tax. This involves placing a tax on GHG emissions, with the goal of reducing emissions and generating revenue for the government. The revenue generated from a carbon tax can then be used to fund initiatives that promote sustainability, such as investing in renewable energy infrastructure or providing subsidies for sustainable businesses.



While there may be concerns about the regressive nature of a carbon tax, where the burden falls disproportionately on low-income individuals, there are ways to mitigate this impact. For example, the revenue generated from a carbon tax can be used to provide financial assistance to low-income households, offsetting the potential increase in energy costs.



Another approach to carbon pricing is through a cap-and-trade system, where a limit is placed on the total amount of emissions allowed and companies can buy and sell emission permits. This creates a market for carbon emissions and encourages companies to find the most cost-effective ways to reduce their emissions.



The implementation of carbon pricing has already shown promising results in countries like Australia. In 2012, the Australian government implemented a carbon price of $23 per tonne, which has led to a decrease in emissions and an increase in investment in renewable energy sources. This demonstrates the potential for carbon pricing to drive significant change at the macro level.



In addition to reducing emissions, carbon pricing also has the potential to generate significant revenue for governments. This revenue can then be used to fund initiatives that promote sustainability and mitigate the impacts of climate change. By setting a price for carbon, we can create a more sustainable future for generations to come.





#### 10.3b Strategies to Set a Price for Carbon



There are various strategies that can be used to set a price for carbon, each with its own advantages and disadvantages. In this section, we will explore some of the most commonly used strategies and their potential impact on the economy and the environment.



One of the most straightforward strategies is a carbon tax, where a fixed price is placed on each tonne of carbon emitted. This approach provides a clear and direct incentive for businesses and individuals to reduce their carbon footprint, as they will face higher costs for emitting carbon. However, there are concerns about the regressive nature of a carbon tax, where the burden falls disproportionately on low-income individuals. To address this issue, revenue generated from a carbon tax can be used to provide financial assistance to low-income households, offsetting the potential increase in energy costs.



Another approach is a cap-and-trade system, where a limit is placed on the total amount of emissions allowed and companies can buy and sell emission permits. This creates a market for carbon emissions and encourages companies to find the most cost-effective ways to reduce their emissions. However, this approach can be complex and requires a well-functioning market to be effective. Additionally, there is a risk of market manipulation and the potential for companies to simply pass on the cost of emission permits to consumers.



A third strategy is a hybrid approach, where a carbon tax is combined with a cap-and-trade system. This approach provides the simplicity and direct incentive of a carbon tax, while also allowing for the flexibility and market-based approach of a cap-and-trade system. However, this approach can also be complex and may require significant administrative resources to implement and maintain.



Regardless of the specific strategy used, setting a price for carbon is crucial for achieving significant and rapid reductions in greenhouse gas emissions. It creates a financial incentive for businesses and individuals to transition to more sustainable practices, such as investing in renewable energy sources and implementing energy-efficient technologies. By incorporating a price for carbon into the economy, we can create a more sustainable future for generations to come.





#### 10.3c Case Studies of Carbon Pricing



As discussed in the previous section, setting a price for carbon is a crucial step in reducing greenhouse gas emissions and promoting sustainable business practices. In this section, we will examine some case studies of countries that have implemented carbon pricing strategies and their impact on the economy and the environment.



One notable example is Australia, which implemented a carbon price of $23 per tonne in 2012. This carbon tax was paid by 300 liable entities representing the highest business emitters in the country. The price was set to increase to $25.40 per tonne by 2014-15 and then be determined by the market from 1 July 2015 onwards. This strategy was expected to encourage efficient use of electricity and promote investment in cleaner renewable energy sources such as solar and wind power.



According to Treasury modelling, with a carbon price in place, renewable energy was projected to reach 40% of Australia's energy supply by 2050. In the first 6 months of the carbon tax's operation, there was a noticeable drop in carbon emissions from the electricity sector. This was attributed to a change in the energy mix, with less electricity being sourced from coal and more from renewables such as hydro and wind power. This was seen as a positive indicator that the government's policies to promote cleaner energy were working.



However, in 2014, the carbon pricing legislation was repealed by the Australian government. Since then, carbon emissions from the electricity sector have increased. This highlights the importance of political will and long-term commitment in implementing effective carbon pricing strategies.



Another example is the European Union's Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), which is the largest carbon market in the world. This cap-and-trade system sets a limit on the total amount of emissions allowed and allows companies to buy and sell emission permits. This creates a market for carbon emissions and incentivizes companies to find cost-effective ways to reduce their emissions.



The EU ETS has faced some challenges, such as an oversupply of emission permits and a low carbon price. However, it has also shown promising results, with a 3.8% reduction in emissions from the sectors covered by the system in 2018 compared to the previous year. This demonstrates the potential of cap-and-trade systems in reducing greenhouse gas emissions.



A third example is British Columbia's carbon tax, which was implemented in 2008. This tax started at $10 per tonne and has gradually increased to $40 per tonne in 2019. The revenue generated from this tax is used to reduce other taxes, making it revenue-neutral. This strategy has been successful in reducing emissions while also promoting economic growth. In fact, British Columbia's GDP has grown faster than the rest of Canada's since the implementation of the carbon tax.



In conclusion, these case studies demonstrate the potential of carbon pricing strategies in reducing greenhouse gas emissions and promoting sustainable business practices. While there are challenges and limitations to each approach, it is clear that setting a price for carbon is a crucial step in navigating the path towards a better future.
