### Subsection 2.1a: Simple Linear Regression



Simple linear regression is the most basic form of regression analysis, where there is a single scalar predictor variable "x" and a single scalar response variable "y". The goal of simple linear regression is to find the best fitting line that describes the relationship between "x" and "y". This line is represented by the equation:



$$
y = \beta_0 + \beta_1x + \epsilon
$$



where "β"<sub>0</sub> and "β"<sub>1</sub> are the intercept and slope coefficients, respectively, and "ε" is the error term. The values of "β"<sub>0</sub> and "β"<sub>1</sub> are estimated using a method called least squares, which minimizes the sum of squared errors between the observed values of "y" and the predicted values from the regression line.



The assumptions underlying simple linear regression include linearity, independence, normality, and homoscedasticity. Linearity assumes that the relationship between "x" and "y" can be described by a straight line. Independence assumes that the observations are not influenced by each other. Normality assumes that the error term "ε" follows a normal distribution. Homoscedasticity assumes that the variance of the error term is constant for all values of "x".



Simple linear regression can be extended to multiple linear regression, where there are multiple independent variables. The basic model for multiple linear regression is:



$$
y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1x_{i1} + \beta_2x_{i2} + ... + \beta_px_{ip} + \epsilon_i
$$



where "y"<sub>i</sub> is the "i"<sup>th</sup> observation of the dependent variable, "x"<sub>ij</sub> is the "i"<sup>th</sup> observation of the "j"<sup>th</sup> independent variable, and "β"<sub>j</sub> represents the parameters to be estimated. This model allows us to examine the relationship between multiple independent variables and a single dependent variable.

Omitted variable bias occurs when important variables are not included in the regression model, leading to biased estimates. Multicollinearity occurs when there is a high correlation between independent variables, which can make it difficult to interpret the individual effects of each variable. Heteroscedasticity occurs when the variance of the error term is not constant for all values of "x", which can affect the accuracy of the estimated coefficients.



In conclusion, regression analysis is a powerful tool for understanding the relationship between variables and can be applied in various contexts, including industrial organization and public policy. ### Section 3.1: Understanding Monopoly



A monopoly is a market structure in which a single firm has complete control over the production and sale of a particular good or service. This gives the firm significant market power, as it is the only supplier in the market and has the ability to set prices and output levels without facing competition. In this section, we will explore the characteristics of a monopoly and the sources of its market power.



#### 3.1a: Characteristics of a Monopoly



A monopoly has at least one of the following five characteristics:



1. Single seller: A monopoly is characterized by a single seller who controls the entire market for a particular good or service. This means that there are no close substitutes for the product and consumers have no choice but to purchase from the monopolist.



2. Unique product: A monopoly produces a unique product that has no close substitutes. This gives the firm a significant amount of market power, as consumers have no alternative options to turn to.



3. High barriers to entry: Monopolies are able to maintain their market power due to the presence of high barriers to entry. These barriers can be economic, legal, or deliberate in nature and prevent potential competitors from entering the market.



4. Price maker: As the sole producer in the market, a monopoly has the power to set prices for its product. This is in contrast to a price taker, which is a firm in a perfectly competitive market that has no control over the market price.



5. Abnormal profits: Due to its market power, a monopoly is able to earn abnormal profits in the long run. This is because the firm is able to charge a price that is higher than its marginal cost, resulting in a larger profit margin.



### Sources of Monopoly Power



Monopolies derive their market power from barriers to entry, which prevent or greatly impede potential competitors from entering the market. These barriers can be economic, legal, or deliberate in nature.



Economic barriers to entry include factors such as high start-up costs, economies of scale, and control over essential resources. For example, a monopoly in the telecommunications industry may have control over the necessary infrastructure, making it difficult for new firms to enter the market.



Legal barriers to entry are created by government regulations and policies. These can include patents, licenses, and other forms of government protection that give the monopolist exclusive rights to produce and sell a particular product.



Deliberate barriers to entry are created by the monopolist itself in order to maintain its market power. This can include tactics such as predatory pricing, where the monopolist lowers prices to drive competitors out of the market, and exclusive contracts with suppliers or distributors.



In addition to barriers to entry, barriers to exit can also contribute to a firm's market power. Barriers to exit are market conditions that make it difficult or expensive for a company to end its involvement with a market. This can include high liquidation costs or long-term contracts that make it difficult for a firm to exit the market even if it is no longer profitable.



### Monopoly versus Competitive Markets



While monopoly and perfect competition are at opposite ends of the market structure spectrum, there are some similarities between the two. Both types of markets aim to minimize costs and maximize profits, and both assume perfectly competitive factor markets.



However, there are also significant differences between a monopoly and a perfectly competitive market. The most significant distinction is that a monopoly has a downward-sloping demand curve, while a perfectly competitive market has a perfectly elastic demand curve. This means that a monopoly has a distinct marginal revenue curve, which is not the case for a perfectly competitive firm.



This distinction has important implications for the behavior of monopolies. For example, a monopoly will produce at a quantity where marginal revenue equals marginal cost, rather than where price equals marginal cost as in a perfectly competitive market. This results in a higher price and lower quantity produced in a monopoly compared to a perfectly competitive market.



In conclusion, monopolies have significant market power due to their ability to control prices and output levels. Payoffs represent the outcomes or rewards that players receive based on the strategies they choose. In game theory, payoffs are typically represented by a numerical value, which can be positive or negative.



#### Nash Equilibrium



A Nash equilibrium is a set of strategies where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy. In other words, each player's strategy is the best response to the other players' strategies. Nash equilibria are important in game theory as they represent stable outcomes in a game.



### Play



In a game, players make decisions simultaneously, without knowledge of the other players' choices. This creates a strategic interaction where each player's decision affects the other players' payoffs. The game is played until a certain outcome is reached, which can be a Nash equilibrium or a suboptimal outcome.

### Subsection 4.1b: Nash Equilibrium



Nash equilibrium is a fundamental concept in game theory and is used to analyze strategic interactions between rational decision-makers. It is named after John Nash, who first introduced the concept in his 1950 paper "Equilibrium Points in N-Person Games."



In a game, a Nash equilibrium is a set of strategies where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy. This means that each player's strategy is the best response to the other players' strategies. In other words, no player has an incentive to deviate from their chosen strategy, given the strategies chosen by the other players.



To better understand Nash equilibrium, let's consider the game of matching pennies. In this game, two players simultaneously choose to either show a "heads" or "tails" side of a coin. If both players show the same side, player 1 wins and receives a payoff of 1, while player 2 receives a payoff of -1. If the players show different sides, player 2 wins and receives a payoff of 1, while player 1 receives a payoff of -1.



In this game, the only Nash equilibrium is for both players to randomly choose "heads" or "tails" with equal probability. This means that neither player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy. If player 1 decides to always choose "heads," player 2 can simply choose "tails" and receive a higher payoff. Similarly, if player 2 decides to always choose "tails," player 1 can choose "heads" and receive a higher payoff.



However, it is important to note that not all rationalizable equilibria are Nash equilibria. Rationalizability is a concept that generalizes Nash equilibrium and refers to the set of strategies that are consistent with a player's beliefs about the other players' strategies. In the game of matching pennies, all pure strategies are rationalizable, but only the mixed strategy Nash equilibrium is the true equilibrium.



In conclusion, Nash equilibrium is a powerful concept in game theory that helps us understand the behavior of rational decision-makers in strategic interactions. It is a stable outcome where no player has an incentive to deviate from their chosen strategy, given the strategies chosen by the other players.

### Section: 13.1 Predatory Pricing:



Predatory pricing is a controversial strategy used by dominant firms to discourage potential competitors from entering the market. It involves the dominant firm offering goods and services at below-cost rates, leading to a reduction in their short-term profits. This drop in price forces the market price to readjust, making it difficult for smaller firms and new entrants to compete. The dominant firm, with its size and capital, can sustain these short-term losses, creating a game of survival that they are likely to win.



The concept of predatory pricing is split into two stages: predation and recoupment. In the first stage, the dominant firm engages in predation by offering goods and services at below-cost rates. This leads to a reduction in their immediate short-term profits, but it also forces the market price to readjust to this lower price. This puts smaller firms and new entrants at risk of exiting the market, as they cannot compete with the dominant firm's low prices.



The second stage, known as recoupment, involves the dominant firm readjusting its prices to approach monopoly prices in the long-term. This allows them to recover their losses from the first stage and maintain their dominant position in the market. This price adjustment can harm consumers, as they are forced to accept higher prices without any fair-priced competition. This is what differentiates predatory pricing from normal competitive pricing.



Under EU law, the European Commission considers recoupment as a factor when determining whether predatory pricing is abusive. This is because predatory pricing can only be considered economically effective if the firm can recover its short-term losses from pricing below Average Variable Costs (AVC). However, recoupment is not a precondition for establishing whether predatory pricing is an abuse of dominance under Article 102 TFEU. Other factors, such as barriers to entry, can also demonstrate how predatory pricing can lead to the foreclosure of competitors from the market.



One of the main concerns with predatory pricing is its potential to harm competition and consumer welfare. By driving out smaller firms and new entrants, the dominant firm can establish a monopoly or dominant position in the market, leading to higher prices and reduced consumer choice. This can also stifle innovation and limit the potential for new and innovative products to enter the market.



To understand predatory pricing better, it is essential to examine the economic models used to analyze this strategy. One such model is the "limit pricing" model, which suggests that the dominant firm sets a low price to deter entry and maintain its dominant position in the market. Predatory pricing is a controversial strategy used by dominant firms to discourage potential competitors from entering the market. It involves the dominant firm offering goods and services at below-cost rates, leading to a reduction in their short-term profits. This drop in price forces the market price to readjust, making it difficult for smaller firms and new entrants to compete. The dominant firm, with its size and capital, can sustain these short-term losses, creating a game of survival that they are likely to win.



The concept of predatory pricing is split into two stages: predation and recoupment. In the first stage, the dominant firm engages in predation by offering goods and services at below-cost rates. This leads to a reduction in their immediate short-term profits, but it also forces the market price to readjust to this lower price. This puts smaller firms and new entrants at risk of exiting the market, as they cannot compete with the dominant firm's low prices.



The second stage, known as recoupment, involves the dominant firm readjusting its prices to approach monopoly prices in the long-term. This allows them to recover their losses from the first stage and maintain their dominant position in the market. This price adjustment can harm consumers, as they are forced to accept higher prices without any fair-priced competition. This is what differentiates predatory pricing from normal competitive pricing.



Under EU law, the European Commission considers recoupment as a factor when determining whether predatory pricing is abusive. This is because predatory pricing can only be considered economically effective if the firm can recover its short-term losses from pricing below Average Variable Costs (AVC). However, recoupment is not a precondition for establishing whether predatory pricing is an abuse of dominance under Article 102 TFEU. Other factors, such as barriers to entry, can also demonstrate how predatory pricing can lead to foreclosure of competitors from the market.

The second stage, recoupment, involves the dominant firm readjusting its prices to approach monopoly prices in the long-term.
