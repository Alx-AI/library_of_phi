A moral person is someone who possesses strong moral values and acts in accordance with them. A moral manager is someone who promotes ethical behavior within their organization. A moral leader is someone who sets a positive example for their followers and inspires them to act ethically. Another theory that is often discussed in the context of ethical leadership is the "Transformational Leadership Theory" proposed by James MacGregor Burns. Virtue ethics, on the other hand, focuses on developing moral character and acting in accordance with virtues such as honesty, compassion, and integrity.

### Section: 23.2 Building Next Generation Leaders through Storytelling



In the previous section, we discussed the power of storytelling in leadership and how it can be used to effectively communicate a leader's vision, values, and goals. Now, we will delve deeper into how storytelling can be used to build the next generation of leaders.



As leaders, it is our responsibility to not only lead in the present, but also to prepare and develop future leaders. Storytelling can be a powerful tool in this process, as it allows us to pass down our knowledge, experiences, and lessons to the next generation. Through storytelling, we can inspire and motivate young leaders, while also teaching them important ethical principles and values.



#### 23.2b Fostering Ethical Leadership through Storytelling



One of the key aspects of leadership is ethical decision-making. As leaders, we are constantly faced with ethical dilemmas and it is our responsibility to make decisions that align with our values and principles. Storytelling can be a powerful tool in fostering ethical leadership in the next generation.



Through storytelling, we can share our own experiences of ethical decision-making and the lessons we have learned. This allows young leaders to learn from our mistakes and successes, and develop their own ethical framework. Additionally, we can use stories to highlight the importance of ethical leadership and the impact it can have on individuals and society as a whole.



However, it is important to note that storytelling should not be used as a means to manipulate or justify unethical actions. As leaders, we must be mindful of the stories we tell and ensure that they align with our values and principles. We must also encourage young leaders to critically analyze the stories they hear and make their own ethical decisions.



#### The Role of Authority in Storytelling



As leaders, we hold a position of authority and power. This can be both a blessing and a curse when it comes to storytelling. On one hand, our authority can give weight and credibility to our stories. On the other hand, it can also lead to a power imbalance and potentially influence the interpretation of our stories.



Therefore, it is important for leaders to use their authority responsibly when telling stories. We must be aware of our influence and ensure that our stories are not used to manipulate or coerce others. Instead, we should use our authority to inspire and empower the next generation of leaders through our stories.



#### The Importance of Authenticity and Vulnerability in Storytelling



In order for storytelling to be effective in building the next generation of leaders, it is crucial for leaders to be authentic and vulnerable in their storytelling. This means sharing our true selves and being open about our struggles and failures. By doing so, we can create a deeper connection with our audience and show that leadership is not about being perfect, but about learning and growing.



Furthermore, by being vulnerable, we can also create a safe space for young leaders to share their own stories and experiences. This can foster a sense of community and encourage open and honest communication, which is essential for developing ethical and effective leaders.
