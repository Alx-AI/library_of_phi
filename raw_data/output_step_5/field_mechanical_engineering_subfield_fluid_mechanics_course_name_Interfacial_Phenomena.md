### Section: 8.1 Contact Angle Hysteresis

#### 8.1a Introduction to Contact Angle Hysteresis

Contact angle hysteresis is a phenomenon that arises due to the non-ideal nature of real surfaces. Unlike ideal surfaces, real surfaces do not possess perfect smoothness, rigidity, or chemical homogeneity. These deviations from ideality result in a range of thermodynamically stable contact angles, known as metastable states, which are observed on a nonideal solid. 

The contact angle hysteresis is defined as the difference between the advancing ($\theta_{a}$) and receding ($\theta_{r}$) contact angles. The advancing contact angle is the maximum stable angle, whereas the receding contact angle is the minimum stable angle. When the contact angle is between these two cases, the contact line is considered to be pinned and hysteretic behavior can be observed. 

#### 8.1b Dynamic Wetting and Contact Angle Hysteresis

The motion of a phase boundary, involving advancing and receding contact angles, is known as dynamic wetting. The difference between dynamic and static wetting angles is proportional to the capillary number, $Ca$. When a contact line advances, covering more of the surface with liquid, the contact angle is increased and is generally related to the velocity of the contact line. 

If the velocity of a contact line is increased without bound, the contact angle increases, and as it approaches 180°, the gas phase will become entrained in a thin layer between the liquid and solid. This is a kinetic nonequilibrium effect which results from the contact line moving at such a high speed that complete wetting cannot occur.

$$

One of the key models in the study of interfacial instabilities is the Rayleigh–Taylor instability. This model describes the instability that occurs at the interface between two fluids of different densities when the heavier fluid is on top of the lighter one. The instability is driven by the gravitational force, which tends to move the heavier fluid downwards and the lighter fluid upwards, leading to the formation of complex patterns at the interface.

The mathematical description of the Rayleigh–Taylor instability is based on a set of interfacial conditions. The first condition states that $\Psi_L=\Psi_G$ at $z=0$, which forces $A_L=A_G=A$. The third condition gives us the relation:

$$
c^2\left(\rho_G D\Psi_G-\rho_L D\Psi_L\right)=g\Psi\left(\rho_G-\rho_L\right)-\sigma\alpha^2\Psi.
$$

By plugging the solution into this equation, we obtain:

$$
c^2=\frac{g}{\alpha}\frac{\rho_L-\rho_G}{\rho_L+\rho_G}+\frac{\sigma\alpha}{\rho_L+\rho_G}.
$$

This equation provides a critical insight into the behavior of the system. In the case of zero surface tension ($\sigma=0$), we have:

$$
c^2=\frac{g}{\alpha}\frac{\rho_L-\rho_G}{\rho_L+\rho_G},
$$

which clearly indicates that the instability is driven by the difference in densities between the two fluids and the gravitational force.

When the heavier fluid is on top, $c^2<0$, and we have:

$$

$$

where $\mathcal{A}$ is the Atwood number. The solution has the form:

$$

and this is associated to the interface position "η" by: $c\eta=\Psi$. Now define $B=A/c$.

$$

$$

which grows exponentially in time. Here "B" is the amplitude of the initial disturbance.

#### 16.1b Interfacial Shear Rheology

In interfacial shear rheology, the interfacial area remains constant throughout the measurement. Instead, the interfacial area is sheared to measure the surface stress present. The equations used in interfacial shear rheology are similar to those used in dilatational interfacial rheology, but the shear modulus is often denoted with G instead of E. In general, G and E are not equal.

Interfacial rheological properties are relatively weak, which presents challenges for measurement equipment. To achieve high sensitivity, it is essential to maximize the contribution of the interface while minimizing the contribution of the bulk phase. The Boussinesq number, Bo, is a dimensionless number that depicts how sensitive a measurement method is for detecting interfacial viscoelasticity. It is given by the equation:

$$
Bo = \frac{G_{interface}}{G_{bulk}}
$$

where $G_{interface}$ is the shear modulus of the interface and $G_{bulk}$ is the shear modulus of the bulk phase.

Commercially available measurement techniques for interfacial shear rheology include the magnetic needle method, the rotating ring method, and the rotating bicone method. The magnetic needle method, developed by Brooks et al., has the highest Boussinesq number of the commercialized methods. In this method, a thin magnetic needle is oscillated at the interface using a magnetic field. The movement of the needle is tracked with a camera, allowing the viscoelastic properties of the interface to be detected. This method is often used in combination with a Langmuir trough to conduct the experiment as a function of the packing density of the molecules or particles.

#### 16.1c Interfacial Dilatational Rheology

Interfacial dilatational rheology is another important aspect of interfacial rheology. Unlike interfacial shear rheology, where the interfacial area remains constant, in dilatational rheology, the interfacial area is subjected to periodic changes in area. This is achieved by compressing and expanding the interface, which allows for the measurement of the surface tension as a function of the surface area.

The dilatational modulus, $E$, is a key parameter in dilatational rheology. It is defined as the change in surface tension with respect to the change in surface area, and is given by the equation:

$$
E = -A \frac{d\gamma}{dA}
$$

where $A$ is the surface area and $\gamma$ is the surface tension. The negative sign indicates that an increase in surface area leads to a decrease in surface tension.

Dilatational rheology is particularly relevant in systems where the interfacial area changes significantly, such as in foams and emulsions. Understanding the dilatational rheological properties can provide insights into the stability of these systems, as well as their response to external forces.

#### 16.1d Techniques for Measuring Interfacial Rheology

There are several techniques available for measuring interfacial rheology, each with its own advantages and limitations. 

1. **Oscillating Drop Method**: This technique involves oscillating a droplet in a surrounding fluid and observing the changes in its shape. The oscillation frequency and amplitude provide information about the interfacial rheological properties. This method is suitable for both shear and dilatational rheology.

2. **Pendant Drop Method**: In this method, a droplet is suspended from a syringe and the changes in its shape under the influence of gravity are observed. This method is primarily used for dilatational rheology.

3. **Interfacial Shear Rheometer**: This device measures the shear stress and strain at the interface by applying a rotational motion. It is most suitable for interfacial shear rheology.

4. **Bicone Interfacial Rheometer**: This technique involves placing a bicone probe at the interface and rotating it to induce shear stress. The resulting deformation provides information about the interfacial rheological properties.

Each of these techniques requires careful setup and calibration to ensure accurate measurements. Furthermore, the choice of technique depends on the specific requirements of the system under study, such as the type of interface (liquid-liquid, liquid-gas, etc.), the nature of the interfacial layer (surfactant, protein, etc.), and the type of deformation (shear, dilatational, etc.).
