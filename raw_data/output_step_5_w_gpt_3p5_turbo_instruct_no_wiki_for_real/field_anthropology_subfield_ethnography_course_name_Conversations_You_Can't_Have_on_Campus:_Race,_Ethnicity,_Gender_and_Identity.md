### Section: 20.2 Disability Rights and Advocacy

#### The Fight for Disability Rights

The fight for disability rights and advocacy has been ongoing for decades. In the United States, the disability rights movement gained momentum in the 1960s and 1970s, with the passing of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 and the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) in 1975. These laws aimed to protect the rights of individuals with disabilities and provide equal access to education and employment opportunities.

In 1990, the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) was passed, which prohibits discrimination against individuals with disabilities in all areas of public life, including education, employment, transportation, and public accommodations. This landmark legislation has greatly improved the lives of individuals with disabilities, but there is still much work to be done in terms of accessibility and inclusion.

#### Disability Advocacy on College Campuses

College campuses have a responsibility to provide equal access and accommodations for students with disabilities. This includes physical accessibility, such as ramps and elevators, as well as accommodations for academic materials and classroom participation. However, many campuses still struggle with providing these accommodations and addressing the needs of students with disabilities.

Disability advocacy groups on college campuses play a crucial role in advocating for the rights and needs of students with disabilities. These groups work to raise awareness, educate the campus community, and push for policy changes to improve accessibility and inclusion. They also provide support and resources for students with disabilities, helping them navigate the challenges they may face on campus.

#### The Intersection of Disability and Other Identities

It is important to recognize that disability does not exist in a vacuum. Many individuals with disabilities also identify with other marginalized identities, such as race, ethnicity, gender, and sexual orientation. This intersectionality can compound the challenges and discrimination faced by individuals with disabilities.

For example, individuals with disabilities who are also people of color may face additional barriers in accessing education and employment opportunities due to systemic racism and ableism. Similarly, individuals with disabilities who identify as LGBTQ+ may face discrimination and lack of support from both the disability community and the LGBTQ+ community.
