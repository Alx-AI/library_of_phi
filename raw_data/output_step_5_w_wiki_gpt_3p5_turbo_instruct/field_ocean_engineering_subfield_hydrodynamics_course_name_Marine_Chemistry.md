NPP is the rate at which autotrophs produce net useful chemical energy, which is then available for consumption by other organisms.

Both GPP and NPP are typically expressed in units of mass per unit area per unit time interval. In the ocean, the most commonly used unit of measurement is grams of carbon per square meter per year (g C m<sup>-2</sup> yr<sup>-1</sup>). #### 8.1a: Causes and Consequences of Ocean Acidification

The primary cause of ocean acidification is the absorption of excess carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) from the atmosphere into the ocean. This process is known as the ocean's "carbon sink," as it helps to regulate the amount of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere. However, with the increasing levels of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from human activities, the ocean's ability to absorb and buffer this excess CO<sub>2</sub> is being overwhelmed. This results in a decrease in the ocean's pH, making it more acidic.

The decrease in pH is due to the chemical reaction between CO<sub>2</sub> and water, which produces carbonic acid (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>). This acid then dissociates into a bicarbonate ion (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup>) and a hydrogen ion (H<sup>+</sup>). The presence of free hydrogen ions (H<sup>+</sup>) lowers the pH of the ocean, making it more acidic.

The consequences of ocean acidification are far-reaching and potentially harmful to marine organisms. One of the most significant impacts is the reduced calcification of marine organisms, such as mollusks and corals. These organisms rely on calcium carbonate to build their shells and skeletons, but the increased acidity of the ocean makes it more difficult for them to do so. This can lead to weakened shells and skeletons, making these organisms more vulnerable to predators and environmental stressors.

Additionally, ocean acidification can also depress metabolic rates, lower immune responses, and reduce energy for basic functions such as reproduction in marine organisms. This can have cascading effects on the entire ocean ecosystem, as these organisms play crucial roles in maintaining the balance and health of the ocean.

It is essential to note that the effects of ocean acidification are not uniform across all regions of the ocean. Factors such as ocean depth, location, and temperature can influence the severity of ocean acidification. For example, colder and higher latitude waters are capable of absorbing more CO<sub>2</sub>, making them more susceptible to increased acidity. One of the most fascinating and important chemical processes in the deep sea is the formation of hydrothermal vents. These vents are found along mid-ocean ridges, where tectonic plates are spreading apart. As the plates move apart, magma rises from the Earth's mantle and creates new oceanic crust. This process, known as seafloor spreading, also creates fractures in the crust, allowing seawater to seep in.

As the seawater comes into contact with the hot magma, it is heated to high temperatures and becomes enriched with various minerals and gases. This superheated water then rises back to the seafloor, forming hydrothermal vents. These vents can reach temperatures of up to 400°C and release a variety of chemicals, including hydrogen sulfide, methane, and carbon dioxide.

The chemical reactions that occur at hydrothermal vents are essential for sustaining life in the deep sea. ### Subsection: 17.1a Sediment Diagenesis

Diagenesis is the process by which sediments are converted into solid rock. In the context of sediment geochemistry, it refers to the chemical and physical changes that occur in sediments after they have been deposited on the ocean floor. These changes can significantly alter the composition and properties of the sediments, and thus have a significant impact on the overall geochemistry of the ocean.

One of the key processes involved in sediment diagenesis is the precipitation or dissolution of silicon (Si). The δ<sup>30</sup>Si value of sediment porewater can be affected by these processes, leading to changes in the isotopic composition of the sediments. The extent and isotopic fractionations of these processes are crucial to understand, as they can alter the δ<sup>30</sup>Si values of the originally deposited sediments and influence the δ<sup>30</sup>Si preserved in the rock record.

Studies have shown that precipitation of Si in sediments tends to prefer the lighter isotope, <sup>28</sup>Si, leading to an enrichment of <sup>30</sup>Si in the dissolved Si in the hosting solution. However, the isotopic effect of Si dissolution in porewater is still not fully understood, as some studies have reported a preference for <sup>28</sup>Si during dissolution, while others have found no significant isotopic fractionation.

Another important aspect of sediment diagenesis is the conversion of iron hydroxide and silica gels into banded iron formations (BIFs). BIFs are a type of sedimentary rock that is rich in iron and silica, and they are thought to have formed through the diagenesis of sediments with similar chemical compositions. The oxidation of ferrous to ferric iron and the precipitation of iron and silica gels as BIFs is an example of diagenesis in action.

The chemical homogeneity and lateral uniformity of BIFs in the Hamersley Range suggest that diagenesis likely left the composition of these sediments unaltered, with the exception of dehydration and decarbonization of the original ferric hydroxide and silica gels. This process may also account for the lack of carbon and abundance of magnetite in older BIFs. The high content of hematite in Neoproterozoic BIFs is thought to be a result of the increased availability of oxygen in the ocean during this time period.

#### 18.1a Isotope Fractionation

Radioactive isotopes play a significant role in the chemical processes of the ocean. These isotopes, also known as radionuclides, are atoms with unstable nuclei that undergo radioactive decay, emitting radiation in the form of alpha, beta, or gamma particles. In this section, we will explore the various natural and anthropogenic radioactive isotopes found in the ocean and their impact on marine chemistry.

#### 19.1a Natural and Anthropogenic Radioisotopes

There are several natural radioactive isotopes found in the ocean, including potassium-40, carbon-14, and uranium-238. These isotopes are formed through cosmic processes, such as supernovae explosions, and are continuously replenished through the decay of other elements. They can also be introduced into the ocean through weathering and erosion of rocks and sediments.

One of the most abundant natural radioactive isotopes in the ocean is potassium-40. This isotope has a half-life of 1.250×10<sup>9</sup> years and decays into stable argon-40 or calcium-40. The decay of potassium-40 to argon-40 is the basis of a common method for dating rocks, known as the K-Ar dating method. This method relies on the assumption that the rocks contained no argon at the time of formation and that all subsequent radiogenic argon was quantitatively retained. By measuring the concentration of potassium and the amount of radiogenic argon in minerals, scientists can determine the age of rocks and sediments.

Apart from dating, potassium isotopes have also been used as tracers in studies of weathering and nutrient cycling in the ocean. Potassium is a macronutrient required for life on Earth, and its isotopes can provide valuable insights into the movement and cycling of nutrients in marine ecosystems.

In addition to natural radioactive isotopes, there are also anthropogenic or man-made isotopes present in the ocean. These isotopes are primarily introduced through nuclear power plants, nuclear weapons testing, and other human activities. Some of the most common anthropogenic isotopes found in the ocean include cesium-137, strontium-90, and plutonium-239. These isotopes can have harmful effects on marine organisms and ecosystems, as they can accumulate in the food chain and cause genetic mutations and other health issues.

It is essential to monitor and study the behavior and distribution of these radioactive isotopes in the ocean to understand their potential impacts on marine life and the environment. Through ongoing research and monitoring, we can better manage and mitigate the effects of these isotopes on the ocean and its inhabitants.
