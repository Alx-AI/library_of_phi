The geostrophic equations, which are a simplified form of the Navier-Stokes equations, describe this balance between the pressure gradient force and the Coriolis effect. These equations assume steady-state, no viscosity, and hydrostatic pressure. They can be written as:

$$
f\rho u = -\frac{\partial p}{\partial x}
$$

$$
f\rho v = -\frac{\partial p}{\partial y}
$$

where $f$ is the Coriolis parameter, $\rho$ is the density, $p$ is the pressure, and $u$ and $v$ are the velocities in the $x$ and $y$ directions, respectively.

One important property of geostrophic currents is that they satisfy the steady-state version of the continuity equation, which states that the net flow into a given area must be equal to the net flow out of that area. This is a useful tool for oceanographers, as it allows them to infer ocean currents from measurements of sea surface height or vertical profiles of seawater density.

Geostrophic currents are found in many of the major currents of the world's oceans, such as the Gulf Stream, the Kuroshio Current, the Agulhas Current, and the Antarctic Circumpolar Current. These currents are all approximately in geostrophic balance and are examples of geostrophic currents.

#### 5.3a Definition of Sverdrup Balance

The Sverdrup balance is a fundamental principle in physical oceanography that describes the relationship between the wind stress and the ocean's circulation. It was first proposed by Norwegian oceanographer Harald Sverdrup in 1947, who derived it from the linearized barotropic vorticity equation for steady motion.

The Sverdrup relation can be written as:

$$
\frac{\partial \tau_y}{\partial z} = -\rho f \frac{\partial V_g}{\partial x}
$$

where $\tau_y$ is the northward wind stress, $\rho$ is the seawater density, $f$ is the Coriolis parameter, and $V_g$ is the geostrophic meridional mass transport.

This equation states that as a vertical column of water is squashed, it moves toward the Equator, and as it is stretched, it moves toward the pole. This is due to the Coriolis effect, which causes the water to deflect to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.

Assuming that there is a level below which motion ceases, the vorticity equation can be integrated from this level to the base of the Ekman surface layer to obtain:

$$
\frac{\partial V_g}{\partial z} = -\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial \tau_y}{\partial z} + \frac{w_E}{\rho}
$$

where $w_E$ is the vertical velocity at the base of the Ekman layer.

The driving force behind the vertical velocity $w_E$ is the Ekman transport, which is caused by the wind stress acting on the ocean surface. In the Northern Hemisphere, the Ekman transport is to the right of the wind stress, while in the Southern Hemisphere, it is to the left. This leads to Ekman divergence (convergence) in regions with a positive (negative) curl of wind stress, and water must rise from beneath to replace the old Ekman layer water. This process is known as "Ekman pumping."

The expression for the Ekman pumping velocity is given by:

$$
w_E = \frac{\tau_x}{\rho f} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} - \frac{\tau_y}{\rho f} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}
$$

Combining this with the previous equation and adding the Ekman transport, we get the Sverdrup relation:

$$
\frac{\partial V_g}{\partial z} = -\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial \tau_y}{\partial z} + \frac{\tau_x}{\rho f} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} - \frac{\tau_y}{\rho f} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + \frac{w_E}{\rho}
$$

In 1948, Henry Stommel proposed a circulation for the entire ocean depth by starting with the same equations as Sverdrup but adding bottom friction. He showed that the variation in the Coriolis parameter with latitude results in a narrow western boundary current in ocean basins.

In 1950, Walter Munk combined the results of Rossby (eddy viscosity), Sverdrup (upper ocean wind-driven flow), and Stommel (western boundary current flow) to propose a complete solution for the ocean circulation. This solution, known as the "Munk-Stommel circulation," is still widely used in physical oceanography today.

To better understand turbulence, we can look at the concept of the Reynolds number. The Reynolds number is a dimensionless quantity that describes the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces in a fluid. In the ocean, a high Reynolds number indicates a turbulent flow, while a low Reynolds number indicates a laminar flow. #### Subsection: 6.4b Turbulence and Ocean Circulation

Turbulence has a significant impact on ocean circulation, which is essential for the transport of heat, nutrients, and other substances. Eddy compensation is a distinct dynamical mechanism from eddy saturation, which is the occurrence of turbulent eddies at the surface. The depth dependence of the circulation in the Southern Ocean leads to a cancellation between the Eulerian circulation and eddy-induced circulation, resulting in a lack of a dynamical link between eddy saturation and eddy compensation. The effects of turbulence on ocean circulation can be seen in the response of the MOC to changes in wind stress.
